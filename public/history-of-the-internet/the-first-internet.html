ARPANET was the network that became the basis for the Internet. Based on a concept first published in 1967, ARPANET was developed under the direction of the U.S. Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA).

ARPANET, together with three networks with different function, became the foundation of modern internet. These networks are:

  1. Military Network (RAND Corporation) based in US
  2. Commercial Network (National Physical Laboratory) based in England
  3. Scientific Network (Cyclades) based in France

ARPANET was an early packet switching network and the first network to implement the protocol suite TCP/IP. Both technologies became the technical foundation of the Internet. There is this main frame that is connected to IMP (Interface Message Processor) which then is connected to IMP subnet, a small network of computers sending data and files. In the IMP subnet, a computer-to-computer connection is made possible by NCP (Network Control Protocol) which later was replaced by TCP (Transmission Control Protocol).

Anything about protocols can be read in Early Protocols page.



The initial function of Internet is for Military. But during 1956 as the demand for its Commercial use in England increased, a lot of files got congested in a traffic during transfer. So Packet Switching was born. This is a mode of data transmission in which a message is broken into a number of parts and sent one by one on any route (as what we have understood in Decentralized Network Architecture) and then being reassembled at the destination.

While then introducing Distributive Method (from World War II page) led to the development of the third focus of communication which is the Scientific Network in France. And this resulted to the birth of Internet where the first message ever sent through ARPANET was in 1986 where Standford tried to send the word "LOGIN" to UCLA but only letters "L" and "O" were successfully sent.

By the end of 1969, only 4 computers (universities) were connected to the network. And as time passed by, Universities from Hawaii Aloha, London, Norway, and etc. were added to the network until by July 1970, there were already 9 computers. But on today's generation, countless number of universities around the globe have an access to the internet with a purpose of reasearching and improving knowledge.



A lot of people had been wanting to connect to the internet so the X.25 protocol, adopted as a standard by the Consultative Committee for International Telegraph and Telephone (CCITT), and a commonly-used network protocol, was developed by phone companies. There was a time in history where people could use either the telephone line or the internet.


There also came DARPA's Transmission Protocols wherein they made use of gateways instead of phone lines.

Another was also OSI (Open System Interconnection Model) designed by ISO (International Organization for Standardization) where in addition to gateways, channels were divided into 7 separate layers with different functions.
